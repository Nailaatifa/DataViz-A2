Kangaroos of Australia

02 · Population of Kangaroo

Just how many kangaroos are there — and how have their numbers shifted over time?

They Outnumber Us

In 2024, Australia is home to roughly 23 million people — and around 35 million kangaroos. Each icon above represents about 5 million individuals. Kangaroos quietly outpace humans by almost 1.5 to 1.

This abundance is no accident: kangaroos are exceptionally adapted to Australia’s dry, open country, and a single mother can carry multiple joeys at different stages of development at the same time.

04 · Species

“Kangaroo” is a colloquial term — biologically, there are many.

One Family, Many Forms

Kangaroos, wallaroos and wallabies all belong to the family Macropodidae — “big foot,” for those famously oversized hind feet. Four species are conventionally called kangaroos: the Red and Antilopine (genus Osphranter) and the Eastern and Western Grey (genus Macropus).

The tree groups species by genus, with population scale and colloquial group encoded by size and colour.

Distribution by State

Where species live varies dramatically by state. Reds dominate the arid interior of NSW, QLD, and SA, while Eastern Greys concentrate along the NSW and QLD seaboard.

Some species are state-exclusive: the Euro (a wallaroo) is only in WA, the Antilopine in NT and QLD, the Yellow-footed Rock Wallaby only in SA and WA.

05 · Characteristics

Bodies tuned for speed, distance, and endurance — built for the outback.

Body Weight

Males are about twice the size of females — a sexual dimorphism that pays off in mate competition. The Red Kangaroo tops out at ~90 kg for males and ~35 kg for females, while wallabies and wallaroos sit far lower on the scale.

Height & Tail

Standing height and tail length scale tightly with body weight — bigger species need longer counter-weights to keep their hopping balanced. A Red Kangaroo male can stand 1.5 m tall and trail a metre-long tail behind him.

Females, again, sit about two-thirds the size of males across every species.

Jumping & Hopping

Bars show the maximum single-hop distance per species; the number above each bar is the top sprint speed (km/h). The Red Kangaroo clears 9 m in a single hop at full sprint — the longest jump of any large mammal on the planet.

06 · Reproduction

A reproductive strategy as unusual as the animal itself.

A Unique Reproductive Process

A short, 30–36 day gestation produces a tiny, underdeveloped joey. It crawls into its mother’s pouch and continues to develop there for several months.

A mother can have a joey in the pouch and another at-foot at the same time — and pause embryonic development entirely if drought strikes. It’s reproduction tuned for a harsh, unpredictable continent.

Mortality by Life Stage

The first six months in the pouch are the most dangerous — roughly half of all joeys don’t make it. Survival improves sharply once the joey is at-foot, and again as it reaches adulthood.

Old age pushes risk back up: senior kangaroos (10+ years) face higher mortality from injury, slowed reflexes, and habitat pressure.

07 · Kangaroo Management

Abundance brings responsibility — Australia’s regulated harvest is a balancing act between population control and animal welfare.

Harvest vs Quota

Each year, state governments set a sustainable quota — the maximum legal harvest based on population estimates. The chart compares that ceiling with the kangaroos actually harvested.

Across every year shown, the actual harvest sits well below the quota — typically only about a quarter of what’s permitted. The quota is a safety margin; the harvest itself tracks demand and conditions on the ground.
