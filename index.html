Population of Kangaroo

Overview

Over the past decade, kangaroo populations in Australia tell a story of growth, declines, and gradual recovery. Eastern Grey and Red Kangaroos consistently dominate the landscape, forming the backbone of the population throughout the years. Around them, Western Grey Kangaroos and Wallaroos maintain a steady presence, while smaller groups like Euro and Tammar Wallaby becoming gradually visible in the data in recent years.

Overall, the kangaroo population rises from about 27 million in 2010 to a peak of over 50 million in the mid-2010s, before settling closer to 35 million by 2024. Rather than a simple rise or fall, the chart reflects a cycle of growth, disruption, and adaptation.

The story reaches its high point between 2014 and 2016, when kangaroo numbers surge to their maximum. This boom likely reflects several years of favorable conditions, where abundant rainfall supported vegetation growth and, in turn, population expansion.

Declining

But the upward trend doesn’t last. From 2019 to 2021, the chart shows a sharp downturn. This period coincides with severe drought and the devastating 2019–2020 Australian bushfire season, which disrupted habitats and reduced populations across multiple species.

Recovery

From 2022 onward, there are signs of resilience. Populations begin to recover, though not to their previous peak levels. Instead, the numbers stabilize at a lower level, suggesting a new balance shaped by recent environmental pressures.

Kangaroos vs Humans

In Australia, it’s not just people shaping the land. Kangaroos are just as much a part of the story. In 2024, their numbers don’t just compete with humans, they outnumber them. For roughly 23 million people, there are about 35 million kangaroos (with some of them not being counted in the survey).

Feral Kangaroos Worldwide

Kangaroo outside Australia (Feral)

As a native species, all kangaroo can only be found in Australia. However, there exist some small feral colonies on other continents — usually escapees from zoos and private collections that have bred in the wild.

According the data from 2024, there are around 5000 feral kangaroos worldwide, with the majority of them are in New Zealand and the United States.

Types of Kangaroo: a genus tree

One family, many forms

Kangaroos, wallaroos and wallabies all belong to the family Macropodidae — “big foot,” for the famously oversized hind feet. Four species are conventionally called kangaroos: the Red and Antilopine (genus Osphranter) and the Eastern and Western Grey (genus Macropus).

The treemap groups species by genus. Each tile is one species, sized by an estimated population, and coloured by colloquial group — kangaroo, wallaroo or wallaby.

Distribution is highly state-dependent — Reds dominate the arid interior of NSW, QLD, and SA, while Eastern Greys are concentrated along the NSW and QLD seaboard.

There exist type of kangaroo that can only be found in specific states. For example, the Euro (a type of wallaroo) is only found in WA, while the Antilopine is only found in NT and QLD. The Black Wallaroo is only found in a small area in the NT, and the Yellow-footed Rock Wallaby is only found in SA and WA.

Female's kangaroo are generally smaller than males. According to the chart, females typically weigh half as much as males. The Red Kangaroo is the largest species, with males weighing up to 90 kg and females around 35 kg. In contrast, the smaller wallabies and wallaroos weigh significantly less, with females often weighing less than 20 kg. This sexual dimorphism is common in kangaroos and is linked to their reproductive strategies and social structures, where larger males compete for access to females.

The tail and standing height of a kangaroo are closely related to its weight. Heavier kangaroos tend to have longer tails and greater standing heights, as these physical traits support their powerful hind legs and enable them to balance and move efficiently. The chart shows a clear positive correlation between weight, tail length, and standing height across different species of kangaroo, with larger species like the Red Kangaroo exhibiting the greatest measurements in all three dimensions.

An Unique Reproductive Process

The lifecycle of a kangaroo can be divided into several stages: gestation, pouch development, and independence.

After a short gestation period of about 30-36 days, underdeveloped joey is born. The joey then crawls into its mother's pouch, where it continues to develop and nurse for several months.

The mother kangaroo can have multiple joeys at different stages of development, with one in the pouch and another at foot, ready to enter the pouch when the first joey leaves.

This reproductive strategy allows kangaroos to quickly adapt to changing environmental conditions, as they can pause the development of a new joey if resources are scarce, and resume it when conditions improve.
