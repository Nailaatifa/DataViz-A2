Kangaroos of Australia

An exploration of the iconic Australian marsupial — populations, distributions, and it's incredible physical prowess.

01 · Australian’s Perception of Kangaroo

Every story of the kangaroo begins here — in how the people who share its land see it.

Australians most associate kangaroos with being “native,” “wildlife,” and a symbol of Australia. The chart also surfaces traits like “muscular” and “energetic,” reflecting their strength and agility — perceptions that set the stage for everything that follows: where they live, how many of them there are, and what makes them physically remarkable.

02 · One Family, Many Forms

“Kangaroo” is a colloquial term — biologically, there are many.

Kangaroos, wallaroos and wallabies all belong to the family Macropodidae — “big foot,” for those famously oversized hind feet. The kangaroos sit in two genera: the Red Kangaroo and Common Wallaroo (genus Osphranter) and the Eastern and Western Greys (genus Macropus).

The tree groups species by genus, with population scale and colloquial group encoded by size and colour.

03 · Population of Kangaroo

Just how many kangaroos are there — and how have their numbers shifted over time?

Population Over Time

Over the past decade, kangaroo numbers tell a story of growth, disruption, and recovery. Eastern Grey and Red Kangaroos form the backbone of the population.

The line traces a rise from ~27 million in 2010 to a peak above 50 million in the mid-2010s, before settling near 35 million by 2024 — a cycle of growth, disruption, and adaptation.

They Outnumber Us

In 2024, Australia is home to roughly 23 million people — and around 35 million kangaroos. Each icon above represents about 5 million individuals. Kangaroos quietly outpace humans by almost 1.5 to 1.

This abundance is no accident: kangaroos are exceptionally adapted to Australia’s dry, open country, and a single mother can carry multiple joeys at different stages of development at the same time.

04 · Habitat & Distribution

Where do all those kangaroos actually live — and why there?

Where the Kangaroos Live

Kangaroos can only be found in Australia. The shaded area on the map shows known habitat zones — mostly forested regions of the eastern and southern states, plus pockets of the arid interior.

Each circle represents an estimated population in a habitat zone.

Distribution by State

Where species live varies dramatically by state. Reds dominate the arid interior of NSW, QLD, and SA, while Eastern Greys concentrate along the NSW and QLD seaboard.

Common Wallaroos hold steady across NSW and QLD’s ranges, while the Tammar Wallaby is largely confined to southern states and offshore islands.

05 · Characteristics

Bodies tuned for speed, distance, and endurance — built for the outback.

Body Weight

Males are about twice the size of females — a sexual dimorphism that pays off in mate competition. The Red Kangaroo tops out at ~90 kg for males and ~35 kg for females, while wallabies and wallaroos sit far lower on the scale.

Height & Tail

Standing height and tail length scale tightly with body weight — bigger species need longer counter-weights to keep their hopping balanced. A Red Kangaroo male can stand 1.5 m tall and trail a metre-long tail behind him.

Jumping & Hopping

Each species sits between two dots: the green marker is its vertical jump height, the brown marker is its horizontal hop distance. The gap between them tells the story — Reds clear 9 m forward but only ~3 m up, a signature of long-range cruising over leaping.

06 · Lifecycle & Survival

A reproductive strategy as unusual as the animal itself.

A Unique Reproductive Process

A short, 30–36 day gestation produces a tiny, underdeveloped joey. It crawls into its mother’s pouch and continues to develop there for several months.

A mother can have a joey in the pouch and another at-foot at the same time — and pause embryonic development entirely if drought strikes. It’s reproduction tuned for a harsh, unpredictable continent.

Mortality by Life Stage

The first six months in the pouch are the most dangerous — roughly half of all joeys don’t make it. Survival improves sharply once the joey is at-foot, and again as it reaches adulthood.

Old age pushes risk back up: senior kangaroos (10+ years) face higher mortality from injury, slowed reflexes, and habitat pressure.

07 · Kangaroo Management

Abundance brings responsibility — Australia’s regulated harvest is a balancing act between population control and animal welfare.

Capacity vs Actual Harvest

Each State bar on the left represents its full sustainable quota — the capacity to harvest. Coloured ribbons trace the portion actually used through Species and on to End-use. The pale ribbon peeling off each state is the unused quota: capacity that was permitted but never harvested.

NSW and QLD have the largest quotas and the largest unused shares — only ~30–35% of what's permitted is taken. The Red and Eastern Grey carry most of the actual harvest, and the bulk of it flows into pet food, followed by meat and skins. A small share is harvested purely for damage mitigation.
