Australian's Perception of Kangaroo

The kangaroo is important to both Australian culture and the national image.

Referring to the National Survey Report by Kangaroos Alive, 83% of Australians associate kangaroos with being an "Australian icon," with 77% identifying them as "native". The top responses are all about national identity.

Another major thread in public perception is "wildlife", kangaroos are found widely across Australia's natural landscape, and as a uniquely native marsupial they exist in the wild almost exclusively on this continent. That association with untouched Australian nature is part of what cements their status as a national symbol.

Unique Australian Marsupials

Kangaroos belong to a lineage found almost nowhere else on Earth.

Kangaroos sit in the infraclass Marsupialia, alongside koalas, wombats, and the Tasmanian devil, a lineage that ended up almost exclusively Australian for two reasons:

Drift & isolation makes all these marsupials trapped inside Australia.

No competition from placental mammals (bears, lions, deer) that never reached the continent.

Population of Kangaroo

Just how many kangaroos are there and how have their numbers shifted?

Figures tracked by the Australian Government's Department of Climate Change, Energy, the Environment and Water (DCCEEW) show a rise from 27 million in 2010 to a peak above 50 million in 2014.

However, in the years since, the trend has shown a steady decline, with the most notable drop in 2021 from ~42 million to ~32 million. The number rose again in 2023, with the overall trend remaining higher than when kangaroos were first recorded.

Habitat & Distribution

From the arid Red Centre to coastal scrub on Kangaroo Island, each species claims its own corner of the continent.

The Australian Range

Native only to Australia, kangaroos occupy a wide spread of environments, from open grasslands and arid deserts to eucalyptus woodlands and rocky hill country.

Distribution skews sharply by state: NSW alone hosts almost 15 million kangaroos, while Victoria is the only state where humans outnumber them.

Notably, the central interior of the continent stays largely empty: the harshest deserts of the Red Centre support almost no kangaroos at all.

Note: Darwin and the Northern Territory look sparse on this map because the NT government does not regularly update its population records.

Red Kangaroo

Most abundant in the arid and semi-arid interior rangelands across WA, SA, NT, QLD, and NSW. Dominates where the country runs driest.

Common Wallaroo

Widespread across every mainland state, hugging the steep escarpments, rocky hills, mountain ranges, and stony rises of rugged semi-arid country.

Tammar Wallaby

Restricted to two pockets of coastal scrub and forest: south-western WA (and nearby islands) and SA's Kangaroo Island.

Eastern Grey Kangaroo

Spans eastern Australia from the Cape York Peninsula in QLD through NSW, VIC, southeastern SA, and into Tasmania. Prefers grass-rich habitats: open woodlands, sclerophyll forests, and semi-arid mallee scrub

Western Grey Kangaroo

Exclusively southern, ranging from coastal WA across SA, VIC, NSW, and into southern QLD. Highly adaptable, thrives in woodlands, open forests, mallee scrubland, and even golf courses and suburban fringes.

Note: VIC reports Eastern and Western Greys as a single "Grey Kangaroo" category.

Characteristics

Body Weight

Belong to the Macropodidae family that means 'big foot' in Latin, Kangaroos are the world's largest marsupials. Red Kangaroo as the biggest, can weight almost 90 kg. In general, males are about twice the size of females.

Height & Tail

Standing height and tail length scale tightly with body weight, where bigger species need longer counter-weights to keep their hopping balanced. A Red Kangaroo male can stand 1.5 m tall with a metre-long tail.

Jumping & Hopping

Supported by their legs stretch and recoil, at higher speeds, kangaroo's hopping requires less energy than running. Even the smallest species, Tammar Wallaby, hop 1.5x further than average human, while Red Kangaroo achieve until 4x of human ability.

Lifecycle & Survival

A reproductive strategy tuned for an unpredictable continent.

1. Gestation: the mother carries a tiny, jelly-bean-sized joey for just a few weeks.

2. Joey in the pouch: the newborn crawls unaided into the pouch and continues developing for months.

3. Pouch exit to weaning: once mature, the joey leaves the pouch and joins the mob.

Females can even pause the development of a second embryo while a joey is still in the pouch; resuming only once it leaves.

Mortality by Life Stage

The pouch is the safer part of kangaroo's lifecycle, the danger spikes the moment joeys emerge. Banks et al. (2000) found around half of newly at-foot young are taken by foxes, making the pouch-exit transition the deadliest window of a kangaroo's life.

Survival climbs steeply once joeys are fully weaned and again as they reach adulthood, where annual mortality drops to roughly 5–10%. Senescence (10+ years) pushes risk back up — vehicle strikes are the dominant cause of adult deaths in studied populations.

Kangaroo Management

Their abundance is considered by some as a threat to biodiversity in conservation reserves, removing these as a haven.

Harvest Flow: Year → State → Species

Each ribbon traces harvested kangaroos through three stages: the year on the left, the state it was harvested in, and the species on the right. Ribbon thickness is the number of animals taken.

QLD and NSW dominate every year, with Eastern grey and Red kangaroo accounting for the vast majority of the take. VIC only enters the regulated harvest in 2021, and the 2020 dip reflects pandemic-era market disruption before the recovery through 2022 – 2023.
